The Second Law of Kirchhoff asserts that a cold, diffuse gas absorbs radiation at the same wavelengths it would emit if it were heated. This principle underpins the formation of absorption lines in the spectra of stars, galaxies, and other objects.

When light from a hot, dense object (like a star) passes through a cooler, diffuse medium (such as a nebula or the outer atmosphere of the star itself), the gas absorbs specific wavelengths of light corresponding to the energies of the transitions between different energy levels of the atoms or molecules in the gas. The resulting spectrum consists of dark absorption lines superimposed on the continuous emission spectrum, known as an absorption spectrum.

These lines, typically seen in the optical and ultraviolet spectra of stars, are crucial in determining the chemical composition and physical conditions of the absorbing gas. The pattern of these absorption lines, along with their strengths, provides a wealth of information about the electron transitions in atoms.
